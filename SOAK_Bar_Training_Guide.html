Bar & Alcohol Training Guide

Welcome to the guide

This guide is for staff who are completely new to working with alcohol and cocktails. You do not need to memorise everything at once. Read through each section, then keep coming back to it as a reference throughout your time at SOAK. By the end, you should be able to describe any drink confidently and make a genuine recommendation when a guest asks.

What is alcohol?

The basics, explained simply

Alcohol in drinks is called ethanol. It is made through a process called fermentation — yeast converts sugar into alcohol. Different ingredients produce different types of drinks. Grapes make wine. Grains make whisky and vodka. Sugarcane makes rum. Agave makes tequila.

Two main categories

Fermented drinks — made by fermenting ingredients and stopping there. Wine and beer fall into this category. They are lower in alcohol, usually between 5% and 15%.

Distilled drinks (spirits) — fermented liquid that is then heated and condensed to increase the alcohol content. Whisky, gin, vodka, rum, and tequila are all spirits. They are much stronger — usually between 37% and 50% alcohol.

What does ABV mean?

ABV stands for Alcohol By Volume. It tells you what percentage of a drink is alcohol. A beer at 5% ABV is mostly water with a small amount of alcohol. A spirit at 40% ABV is much stronger. This is why spirits are served in small measures and mixed with other ingredients.

Why does this matter on the floor?

When a guest asks "is this strong?" or "what does this taste like?", you need to know the basics to give an honest, helpful answer. A guest who orders something expecting a light drink and receives something strong will be disappointed. Your job is to guide them before they order.

How to use this guide: Tap the tabs at the top to move between sections. Start with Spirits, work through Ingredients, Glassware, and Techniques, then spend the most time on Cocktails and Wine — those are what you will use most on the floor.

Supporting ingredients

A cocktail is more than just a spirit. These are the other ingredients you will hear about and need to be able to explain simply to a guest.

Bitters

Bitters are a very concentrated liquid made from herbs, spices, fruit peels, and roots. They are extremely intense — just a few drops are used at a time, never drunk on their own. Think of them like seasoning in food. You don't eat salt by itself, but it makes everything taste better. The most common is Angostura bitters — spiced and aromatic.

How to explain to a guest: "Bitters are a flavouring ingredient — just a few drops that add depth and a slight herbal note. You won't taste them on their own, but they balance the whole drink."

Fresh lemon juice

Bright and acidic. Used in the Whisky Sour, Tom Collins, and Bee's Knees.

Prosecco / Champagne

Sparkling wine used as a mixer in the Aperol Spritz and Bellini. Adds bubbles and lightness.

Garnishes

On the rim or in the glass. Guests can squeeze it in for extra freshness.

Why does the glass matter? A narrow flute keeps sparkling wine bubbly longer. A wide coupe lets the aroma of a shaken cocktail open up. A rocks glass is short and wide — perfect for drinks served over ice that you sip slowly. The copper mug keeps a Moscow Mule cold. As floor staff, you will not choose the glass — the bar team does. But you should be able to tell a guest what glass their drink comes in when they ask.

Cocktail techniques

Every cocktail is made using one of four methods. Knowing the difference helps you describe the drink to a guest and understand what the bar team is doing.

Shaken

What happens

Ingredients are put in a metal shaker with ice and shaken vigorously for 10-15 seconds. The ice chills and dilutes the drink, and the shaking creates a slightly frothy texture.

Why it's done this way

Shaking mixes ingredients that don't naturally combine — like citrus juice, egg white, or cream. It also creates a lighter, slightly aerated texture.

Used for

Daiquiri, Margarita, Cosmopolitan, Whisky Sour, Bee's Knees, Espresso Martini

🍸

Stirred

What happens

Ingredients are combined with ice in a mixing vessel and stirred slowly with a long bar spoon for about 30 seconds.

Why it's done this way

Stirring chills and dilutes the drink gently without adding air bubbles. It keeps the drink clear and silky. Used for spirit-forward cocktails where clarity and texture matter.

Used for

Old Fashioned, Manhattan, Negroni

🥄

Built

What happens

The drink is made directly in the serving glass. Ice goes in first, then the spirit, then the mixer is poured on top.

Why it's done this way

Simple drinks that don't need shaking or stirring are built directly. Faster, and preserves the carbonation of fizzy mixers.

Used for

Gin & Tonic, Highball, Moscow Mule, Vodka Soda, Aperol Spritz, Dark & Stormy

🥂

Blended

What happens

Ingredients are blended with ice in a blender until smooth and frozen.

Why it's done this way

Creates a thick, frozen, slushy texture. Used for tropical cocktails served very cold.

Used for

Frozen Pina Colada, Frozen Daiquiri

Key tools to know

Jigger

A small measuring tool — usually 30ml and 45ml sides. Measures spirits accurately so every drink is consistent.

Shaker

A metal tin used to shake cocktails. Ice and ingredients go in, it is sealed and shaken hard.

Bar spoon

A long, thin spoon for stirring cocktails gently. The long handle keeps the bartender's hand away from the ice.

Muddler

A thick tool used to crush ingredients at the bottom of a glass — mint in a Mojito, lime in a Caipirinha.

Strainer

Placed over the shaker when pouring — catches ice and fruit pulp so they don't go into the final drink.

Speed pourer

A spout placed in the neck of a spirit bottle. Allows smooth, consistent pouring without removing the cap.

Wine

Wine is made from fermented grapes. Unlike spirits, wine is not distilled — which keeps the alcohol lower, usually between 11% and 15%. SOAK serves wine by the glass and by the bottle. You need to know the main categories, the key grape varieties, and how to guide a guest who is unsure what to order.

🍷 Red wine

What it is

Made from dark-coloured grapes. The skin is included during fermentation, giving red wine its colour and tannins — a slight drying sensation in the mouth, like very strong tea.

Taste profile

Ranges from light and fruity (Pinot Noir) to full, rich, and bold (Cabernet Sauvignon). Generally has notes of dark fruit — blackberry, cherry, plum.

Served at

Room temperature or slightly cool. Never chilled.

Key grapes

Cabernet Sauvignon — bold, full-bodied, blackcurrantMerlot — softer, plummy, smoothShiraz / Syrah — spicy, dark fruit, pepperyPinot Noir — light, delicate, cherry notes

Brands at SOAK

Sula Dindori Reserve Shiraz, Fratelli Sette, Jacob's Creek Shiraz Cabernet, Two Oceans Cabernet Sauvignon

Food pairings

Grills, tandoor dishes, red meat, rich pasta

When to recommend

A guest who wants something rich and full-flavoured. For unsure guests — start with Merlot (safe and approachable) or Shiraz (bold and popular in India).

🥂 White wine

What it is

Made from green or yellow grapes, or from dark grapes with the skin removed. Lighter in colour, lower in tannins. Generally crisper and more refreshing than red wine.

Taste profile

Ranges from very dry and crisp (Sauvignon Blanc) to rich and buttery (Chardonnay) to sweet and floral (Moscato).

Served at

Chilled. Always cold when it reaches the guest.

Key grapes

Sauvignon Blanc — dry, crisp, citrusyChardonnay — fuller, buttery, oakyPinot Grigio — light, dry, easy drinkingRiesling — floral, can be sweet or dryMoscato — sweet, low alcohol, fruity

Brands at SOAK

Sula Chenin Blanc, Fratelli Bianco, Jacob's Creek Pinot Grigio, Two Oceans Sauvignon Blanc

Food pairings

Seafood, lighter dishes, salads, grilled fish, coastal Indian food

When to recommend

A guest who wants something light and refreshing. For unsure guests — Pinot Grigio is the safest, most crowd-pleasing white.

🌸 Rosé

What it is

Made from red grapes, but the skin is only in contact with the juice for a short time — giving it a pink colour. It is NOT a mix of red and white wine.

Taste profile

Lighter than red, with more fruit than white. Usually dry, with notes of strawberry, watermelon, and citrus. Refreshing and easy to drink.

Served at

Chilled — like white wine.

Brands at SOAK

Sula Rosé, Fratelli Rosato, Jacob's Creek Rosé

Food pairings

Light snacks, salads, seafood, Mediterranean dishes, bar food

When to recommend

A guest who cannot decide between red and white. Especially popular on the terrace at sunset. Great for guests who want something light, pink, and pretty.

🍾 Champagne

What it is

Sparkling wine made only in the Champagne region of France. True Champagne is expensive — only wine from that specific region can use the name.

Taste profile

Dry, crisp, biscuity, and toasty with fine, persistent bubbles. Very elegant. "Brut" means dry — the most common style.

Served at

Very cold. In a flute to preserve bubbles.

Common houses

Moët & Chandon, Veuve Clicquot, Laurent-Perrier

When to recommend

Celebrations, anniversaries, special occasions. When a guest wants to mark a moment.

🇮🇹 Prosecco

What it is

Italian sparkling wine made from Glera grapes. More affordable than Champagne. The most popular sparkling wine in the world.

Taste profile

Lighter and fruitier than Champagne. Floral, peachy, and slightly sweet. Larger, more playful bubbles. Very approachable.

Served at

Very cold. In a flute. Also used in the Aperol Spritz and Bellini.

Common brands

La Marca, Mionetto, Santa Margherita

When to recommend

A guest who wants something bubbly and celebratory but doesn't want to pay for Champagne. Also for guests who find Champagne too dry.

🇪🇸 Cava

What it is

Spanish sparkling wine made using the same method as Champagne but from Spanish grapes. More affordable than Champagne, with a similar production style.

Taste profile

Drier than Prosecco, slightly earthy and nutty. Closer to Champagne in style — crisp and dry with fine bubbles.

Served at

Very cold. In a flute.

Common brands

Freixenet, Codorníu, Segura Viudas

When to recommend

A guest who wants something dry and bubbly at a more accessible price than Champagne. Good for guests who know their wine.

How to recommend wine to a guest: Start with one question — "Do you prefer red, white, or something bubbly?" If they don't know, ask: "Would you like something light and refreshing, or something richer and fuller?" For groups of three or more, gently suggest a bottle: "A bottle works out to about four glasses — would that work for the table?"

Whisky in Depth

Whisky is one of the most complex and varied spirits categories. Understanding age statements, regional differences, and production styles will help you guide guests through whisky choices with confidence.

🥃 Age statements — what they mean

The number on the bottle

When a whisky says 12 Year, 18 Year, or 25 Year, that number tells you the youngest whisky in the bottle. If a blend contains whisky aged 12, 15, and 18 years, it is called a 12 Year because that is the minimum age.

Why older = more expensive

Whisky ages in wooden barrels. The longer it ages, the more flavour it picks up from the wood — but also the more liquid evaporates (called the "angel's share"). A 25-year-old whisky has lost significantly more volume than a 12-year-old, and it has been sitting in a warehouse taking up space for over two decades. That is why older whiskies cost more.

Flavour and age

Older whiskies are generally smoother, more complex, and more refined. Younger whiskies (no age statement, or 8-10 years) are bolder, sharper, and more vibrant. Neither is better — it depends on the guest's preference.

What to tell a guest

If a guest asks about age: "The number tells you how long the whisky was aged. Older whiskies are smoother and more complex, younger whiskies are bolder and more intense. It comes down to what you prefer."

Single Malt vs Blended Scotch vs Bourbon

Single Malt Scotch

Made at a single distillery in Scotland, using only malted barley. Single malts are prized for their distinctive character — each distillery's whisky tastes different based on water source, barrel type, and local conditions. Examples: Glenfiddich, Glenlivet, Macallan, Laphroaig.

Taste

Varies widely. Can be smooth and fruity (Speyside), rich and sherried (Highlands), or intensely smoky and peaty (Islay). More complex than blended Scotch.

Price

Generally more expensive than blended Scotch because it comes from one distillery and has a unique character.

How to recommend

For whisky enthusiasts who want to taste the distillery's signature style. If a guest says they enjoy whisky and want to explore, single malts are the place to start.

Blended Scotch

A mix of malt whiskies and grain whiskies from multiple distilleries, blended together by a master blender to create a consistent flavour profile. Examples: Johnnie Walker, Chivas Regal, Dewar's, Black & White.

Taste

Smooth, balanced, and consistent. Less complex than single malts but very approachable. The goal of a blend is to taste the same every time.

Price

Ranges from affordable (Johnnie Walker Red) to premium (Johnnie Walker Blue, Chivas Regal 18). Blends are generally more accessible than single malts of the same age.

How to recommend

For guests who want a smooth, reliable whisky without the intensity or variability of a single malt. Great for guests new to Scotch or who drink whisky with mixers.

Bourbon

American whiskey made primarily in Kentucky. It must be made from at least 51% corn and aged in brand new charred oak barrels. The charred wood gives Bourbon its characteristic vanilla, caramel, and sweet flavour. Unlike Scotch which can reuse barrels, Bourbon barrels are only used once — which is why Bourbon tends to be sweeter and richer.

Taste

Sweet, rich, and warm. Strong notes of vanilla, caramel, honey, and sometimes oak spice. Much sweeter than Scotch.

Price

Ranges widely. Jack Daniel's and Jim Beam are everyday Bourbons. Maker's Mark, Buffalo Trace, and Woodford Reserve are premium.

Common brands

Jack Daniel's (technically Tennessee Whiskey, not Bourbon but very similar), Jim Beam, Maker's Mark, Buffalo Trace, Woodford Reserve, Bulleit, Wild Turkey

How to recommend

For guests who find Scotch too smoky or harsh, or who prefer sweeter flavour profiles. Bourbon works very well in an Old Fashioned or Whisky Sour.

Other whisky styles to know

Irish Whiskey

Triple distilled, which makes it exceptionally smooth and light. No peat smoke. Very approachable. Example: Jameson. Best for guests who find Scotch too heavy.

Japanese Whisky

Inspired by Scotch traditions but with a distinctly delicate, precise Japanese style. Very smooth, subtle, and refined. Examples: Suntory Toki, Nikka, Hibiki. Premium and increasingly sought after.

Rye Whiskey

Made primarily from rye grain. Spicier and drier than Bourbon. Used in classic cocktails like the Manhattan and Whisky Sour. Examples: Bulleit Rye, Rittenhouse.

Indian Whisky

Most Indian whisky is technically a spirit made from molasses, not grain — which means it does not qualify as whisky by international standards. Paul John and Amrut are exceptions — genuine Indian single malts made from barley, winning international awards.

IMFL vs FMFL

IMFL and FMFL are terms unique to India's alcohol industry. Understanding the difference helps you explain pricing to guests and understand why some bottles cost significantly more than others.

What do these terms mean?

IMFL — Indian Made Foreign Liquor

Spirits that are manufactured in India but follow foreign production styles. Examples: Johnnie Walker Red Label bottled in India, Smirnoff bottled in India, or Indian brands like Old Monk and Greater Than gin. These bottles are made locally under license or are Indian brands styled after international spirits.

FMFL — Foreign Made Foreign Liquor

Spirits that are made outside India and imported in sealed bottles. Examples: Johnnie Walker Blue Label imported from Scotland, Patrón imported from Mexico, Hendrick's imported from Scotland. These are fully produced and bottled abroad.

Why it matters — price and tax

FMFL bottles are significantly more expensive than IMFL bottles because:

Import duties

India charges very high import taxes on alcohol — sometimes 150% or more of the bottle's value. This is why a bottle of Johnnie Walker Blue Label costs so much more than Johnnie Walker Red Label even though both are from the same company.

Shipping and logistics

Imported bottles have to be shipped internationally, cleared through customs, and handled carefully to avoid breakage. This all adds to the cost.

Perceived quality

Many guests believe FMFL is higher quality because it is imported and costs more. While imported bottles are often premium, some IMFL bottles are excellent quality — it is not a rule that foreign = better.

How to talk about it with guests

Most guests will not ask about IMFL vs FMFL directly. But if a guest questions why a particular bottle costs more:

What to say

"That bottle is imported, so the price includes heavy import duties and shipping costs. We also have excellent locally-made options that offer great value if you'd like a recommendation."

What NOT to say

Never say "this is IMFL so it is cheaper" or "this is FMFL so it is better quality" — these terms sound technical and confusing to most guests. Stick to imported vs locally-made if you need to explain.

Serving Sizes

Understanding pour sizes is essential. Guests will order using terms like "small", "large", "peg", and "double" — you need to know exactly what they mean and what you are pouring.

Standard spirit pour sizes

30ml — Small / Single peg

This is the standard single serving of spirits in India. When a guest orders "a whisky" or "a small", this is what they get. It is enough for one drink, neat or on the rocks, or as the base for a simple mixed drink like a whisky and soda.

60ml — Large / Peg

Double the small. When a guest says "large whisky" or just "a peg", this is what they mean. This is a generous serving — enough for a strong drink or to be enjoyed slowly over ice.

120ml — Double large

Rare but occasionally requested, especially by guests who want to settle in for a long evening. This is a very large serving and should be clarified with the guest before pouring to make sure they understand what they are ordering.

How cocktails are poured

Cocktails follow recipes, not peg sizes. A Mojito contains a specific measured amount of rum based on the recipe — usually 45ml or 60ml depending on the bar's standard. Guests cannot order a "small Mojito" or "large Negroni" in the same way they can order small or large neat spirits.

If a guest asks for a stronger cocktail

Offer to add an extra shot (30ml) to the drink for an additional charge. For example: "Would you like an extra shot of rum in the Mojito? That would be [price]."

Beer sizes

Mug — approximately 300ml

A standard beer mug. Smaller, quicker to finish, good for guests who want a cold beer but not a full pint.

Pint — approximately 475ml

A proper pint glass. The standard serving size in most bars. Good for guests who want a full, satisfying beer.

Common mistakes to avoid

Never assume

If a guest orders "a whisky", clarify whether they want small or large before pouring. A guest who expected small and got charged for large will be unhappy.

Never over-pour

Use a measured pour every time. Free-pouring spirits leads to inconsistent drinks, unhappy guests, and lost revenue.

Never under-pour

If a guest ordered a large and it looks small in the glass, they will notice. Measure correctly and serve confidently.

Common Bar Phrases

Guests use specific language when ordering drinks. Knowing these terms helps you take orders accurately and sound confident and professional.

How spirits are served

Neat

Served at room temperature in a glass with no ice, no water, nothing else. Pure spirit only. Example: "Whisky neat."

On the rocks

Served over ice. No mixers, no water — just spirit and ice. Example: "Whisky on the rocks."

With water

Served neat or on the rocks, with a separate small glass or carafe of still water on the side so the guest can dilute the spirit themselves. Example: "Whisky with water."

Chilled

The bottle or spirit has been stored cold, but served without ice. Rare for whisky, more common for vodka.

Straight up

Shaken or stirred with ice to chill it, then strained into a glass without ice. Used for cocktails like Martinis. Example: "Martini straight up."

Back

A separate small drink served alongside the main drink, usually water or soda. Example: "Whisky neat with a soda back" means serve a whisky neat and a small glass of soda on the side.

Cocktail terms

Dirty

Usually refers to a Dirty Martini — a Martini with olive brine added, giving it a cloudy, salty flavour. Example: "Vodka Martini, dirty."

Dry

Refers to the amount of vermouth in a Martini. A dry Martini has very little vermouth — mostly gin or vodka. The drier it is, the less vermouth.

Virgin / Mocktail

A non-alcoholic version of a cocktail. Example: "Virgin Mojito" means a Mojito with no rum.

Highball

A family of drinks made with a spirit and a larger amount of a non-alcoholic mixer, served in a tall glass. Examples: Whisky Highball (whisky and soda), Gin & Tonic, Vodka Soda.

Float

A small amount of liquid poured gently on top of a drink so it sits on the surface rather than mixing in. Example: "Float Baileys on top of the coffee."

Top up

To add more of a mixer or ingredient to finish the drink. Example: "Top up with soda" means fill the rest of the glass with soda.

Guest behaviour terms

Nightcap

A final drink at the end of the evening before leaving. Usually something smooth and warming — whisky, brandy, or a digestif.

Aperitif

A drink served before a meal to stimulate the appetite. Usually light, dry, and refreshing — Aperol Spritz, Prosecco, dry Martini, Campari.

Digestif

Beer

Beer is fermented from grains — usually barley — and flavoured with hops. Unlike spirits, beer is not distilled, so it has a much lower alcohol content, typically 4–6%. Staff need to know the difference between draught and bottled beer, when to recommend each, and how to talk about beer confidently.

🍺 Draught vs Bottled / Canned beer

What is draught beer?

Beer served from a keg through a tap. The keg is stored cold and pressurised, and the beer is poured fresh into a glass every time. Draught beer is never exposed to light and is kept at a consistent temperature, which keeps it fresh and flavourful.

What is bottled or canned beer?

Beer that has been packaged in bottles or cans at the brewery and sealed. Bottled beer can be affected by light and temperature changes during storage and transport, which can sometimes alter the flavour slightly over time.

Which tastes better?

Draught beer is generally considered fresher and better-tasting because it is never exposed to light, stays cold consistently, and the pouring process creates a natural foam head that releases aroma and makes the beer taste smoother and creamier. Bottled beer is still excellent quality, but it does not have the same freshness or presentation as draught.

When to recommend draught

If a guest wants the freshest beer experience and does not care about a specific brand, draught is always the best recommendation. It also looks better — a properly poured draught beer with a thick foam head is more visually impressive than a bottle on the table.

When to recommend bottled

If a guest asks for a specific brand that is only available in bottles, or if they specifically prefer drinking from the bottle rather than a glass.

Pint vs Mug

Mug — approximately 300ml

A smaller serving. Good for guests who want a quick cold beer but do not want to commit to a full pint. Also good for guests who want to try a beer before ordering a larger size.

Pint — approximately 475ml

The standard beer serving size. A full, satisfying glass. Most guests will order a pint unless they want something smaller.

How to recommend

If a guest orders "a beer" without specifying, ask: "Would you like a pint or a mug?" Do not assume — some guests prefer smaller servings.

How to talk about beer

A guest asks for a beer recommendation

Ask one question first: "Do you prefer something light and easy-drinking, or something with more flavour and body?" Then recommend accordingly. If unsure, draught beer is always a safe recommendation.

A guest asks what the difference is between draught and bottled

"Draught comes fresh from the keg through the tap — it is never exposed to light, stays at a perfect temperature, and the pour creates a natural foam head that makes it taste smoother. Bottled is great too, especially if you want a specific brand. But if you want the freshest experience, draught is what I would recommend."

Pitchers

Pitchers are available for select cocktails and draught beer at SOAK. They are designed for groups and offer better value than ordering individual drinks. Staff should know when to recommend pitchers and how to make the suggestion feel natural and helpful.

What are pitchers?

A pitcher is a large jug containing multiple servings of a cocktail or beer, served with individual glasses for the table to share. Typically contains 4–6 servings depending on the drink.

Which drinks are available as pitchers?

Pitchers are available for select cocktails and draught beer. Check with the bar team or refer to the menu for the current list, as availability may change based on what is being served.

When to recommend pitchers

Groups of 3 or more ordering the same drink

If multiple people at a table order the same cocktail or beer, suggest a pitcher: "Would you like to get a pitcher instead? It works out to about [number] drinks and is better value."

Tables settling in for a long evening

If a table seems relaxed and in no rush, pitchers make sense — they do not have to keep reordering and the drinks stay cold in the jug.

Hot days on the terrace

Pitchers work especially well on warm afternoons when guests want something refreshing they can share without individual glasses warming up quickly.

How to offer a pitcher

Never push. Present it as an option that might work better for the table, not as something they must do.

Good approach

"You are all having Mojitos — would you like a pitcher for the table instead? It is about [X] glasses and works out better value-wise."

What NOT to say

Never say "It is cheaper" — this makes it sound low-quality. Say "better value" instead.

Common pitcher questions

How many drinks are in a pitcher?

Typically 4–6 servings depending on the cocktail or beer. Check with the bar if unsure, but you should know this before offering it to a guest.

Can we get half the pitcher one cocktail and half another?

No. Pitchers are a single drink only. If the table wants variety, individual servings are better.

Can we take the pitcher home if we do not finish?

No — alcohol cannot leave the premises. If the table is concerned about finishing it, suggest starting with individual drinks and ordering a pitcher later if they are still drinking.

Aperitifs & Digestifs

An aperitif is a drink you have before a meal to open up your appetite. A digestif is a drink you have after a meal to aid digestion and close the evening. Understanding these helps you guide guests through the full dining experience, not just serve drinks.

🍹 Aperitifs — before the meal

What is an aperitif?

A light, dry, refreshing drink designed to stimulate your appetite before eating. Aperitifs are usually low in alcohol, crisp, and not too sweet — they prepare your palate for food rather than filling you up.

Common aperitifs

Aperol Spritz, Prosecco, Champagne, dry Martini, Negroni, Campari and soda, dry white wine, Bellini.

Flavour profile

Light, crisp, dry, slightly bitter, refreshing. Nothing heavy, creamy, or overly sweet.

When to recommend

If a guest arrives and is looking at the menu, suggest an aperitif while they decide what to eat: "Would you like something light to start? An Aperol Spritz or a glass of Prosecco?" This sets the tone for a relaxed, thoughtful dining experience.

🥃 Digestifs — after the meal

What is a digestif?

A drink served at the end of a meal to aid digestion and signal the close of the evening. Digestifs are usually stronger, richer, and more warming than aperitifs — they are meant to be sipped slowly.

Common digestifs

Whisky (especially aged whisky), brandy, cognac, Amaro, Fernet-Branca, Espresso Martini, Old Monk neat, port wine, grappa.

Flavour profile

Rich, warming, smooth, sometimes herbal or bitter. Higher in alcohol. Meant to be enjoyed slowly.

When to recommend

After a guest finishes their meal and seems settled in for the evening, offer a digestif: "Would you like something to finish — perhaps a whisky or an Espresso Martini?" This is especially effective if the guest has had a good meal and is relaxed at the table.

How to talk about aperitifs and digestifs

A guest asks what an aperitif is

"An aperitif is a drink you have before your meal — usually something light and refreshing that opens up your appetite. At SOAK, the Aperol Spritz and Prosecco are perfect aperitifs."

A guest asks what a digestif is

"A digestif is something you have after the meal to help with digestion and close out the evening. It is usually something smooth and warming — whisky, brandy, or an Espresso Martini if you want coffee."

A guest does not know these terms but seems interested in pre- or post-meal drinks

You do not need to use the words aperitif or digestif unless the guest uses them first. Simply say: "Would you like something light and refreshing to start?" or "Can I get you something to finish — perhaps a whisky or a coffee-based cocktail?"

Responsible Service of Alcohol

Serving alcohol responsibly is a legal obligation and a core part of SOAK's reputation. Staff must know how to identify guests who have had too much to drink or who appear underage, and most importantly, know that they never handle these situations alone — they inform the MOD immediately and let the MOD take action.

⚠️ Legal drinking age in Tamil Nadu: 21

Anyone who appears to be under 21 cannot be served alcohol without valid government-issued ID proving they are of legal age. If there is any doubt, ID must be checked before serving.

What counts as valid ID?

Passport, driver's license, Aadhaar card (with date of birth), voter ID, or any other government-issued photo ID showing date of birth.

What if a guest does not have ID?

No ID = no alcohol. No exceptions, no matter how insistent the guest is. The MOD will handle the conversation.

How to identify a guest who has had too much

Look for these signs. If you see any combination of them, inform the MOD immediately via walkie talkie:

Slurred speech Unsteady movement or difficulty walking Glassy or unfocused eyes Loud, aggressive, or inappropriate behaviour Repeatedly ordering drinks very quickly Spilling drinks or knocking things over Falling asleep at the table Becoming argumentative with staff or other guests

If you see any of these signs, do NOT serve another alcoholic drink. Pause and inform the MOD discreetly via walkie talkie before returning to the table.

What staff should NEVER do

Never cut off a guest yourself

You are not authorised to refuse service or tell a guest they cannot drink more. Only the MOD makes that decision.

Never argue with an intoxicated guest

If a guest becomes difficult, step back and get the MOD. Do not engage in a back-and-forth.

Never assume a guest is fine because they seem friendly

A guest can be polite and still dangerously intoxicated. Look for the physical signs, not the personality.

Never serve someone who is clearly underage just because they are with older people

Even if the rest of the table is of legal age, every person being served alcohol must be 21 or older with valid ID.

What to do if you spot a problem

Step 1 — Pause

Do not take any more drink orders from that guest or table until you have informed the MOD.

Step 2 — Inform the MOD via walkie talkie

Be discreet and factual. For example: "MOD, I have a situation at table 12 — guest appears very intoxicated, slurred speech, ordering rapidly. Can you come assess?"

Step 3 — Let the MOD handle it

The MOD will come to the table, assess the guest, and decide whether to stop service, offer food and water, or ask the guest to leave. Your job is to flag the situation — the MOD's job is to resolve it.

Step 4 — Continue normal service for the rest of the table

Unless the MOD instructs otherwise, continue serving the other guests at the table normally. Do not make the situation obvious to nearby tables.

Difficult guest scenarios

A guest insists they are fine and demands another drink

Say calmly: "I understand — let me just check with my manager to confirm we can get that for you." Then inform the MOD. Do not argue or refuse directly.

A guest becomes aggressive when you pause service

Step back, stay calm, and get the MOD immediately. Do not escalate. Your safety is more important than any drink order.

A guest asks why their friend is not being served

Say: "The manager is handling it — they will come speak to you in just a moment." Do not explain house policy yourself. Let the MOD manage the conversation.

Real-World Scenarios

Practice handling common bar situations.

Scenario 1: Guest asks for a "strong" drink
Response: "Would you like a double pour, or something with a higher-proof spirit like an Old Fashioned or Negroni?"
Avoid: Free-pouring extra alcohol without charging
Scenario 2: Guest doesn't like their cocktail
Response: "I'm sorry it's not to your taste. Let me make you something else — what flavors do you usually enjoy?"
Avoid: Getting defensive or blaming the guest
Scenario 3: Guest asks what's in a drink
Response: Know your menu. List the base spirit, key ingredients, and flavor profile. Example: "The Margarita has tequila, lime juice, and triple sec — it's citrusy and refreshing."
Scenario 4: Guest is indecisive
Response: Ask guiding questions: "Do you prefer something light and fruity, or strong and spirit-forward?" Then suggest 2-3 options.
Scenario 5: Guest seems intoxicated
Response: Inform your manager immediately. Offer water, food, or mocktails. Do not serve more alcohol. Stay calm and respectful.
Scenario 6: Busy bar, multiple orders
Strategy: Acknowledge everyone ("I'll be right with you!"), serve in order, batch similar drinks, communicate wait times honestly.
Scenario 7: Guest asks for a recommendation
Response: Ask about preferences first, then confidently suggest a drink you know well. Share why you like it. Example: "If you like gin, the Basil Smash is fantastic — fresh, herbal, really balanced."

Final Tip: Confidence comes from practice. Role-play these scenarios with your team until responses feel natural.
