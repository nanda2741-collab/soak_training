Cooking Techniques Training Guide

Welcome to this guide

This guide is for front of house staff who want to understand the basics of how food is cooked. You do not need to cook anything yourself. But knowing what happens in the kitchen means you can describe dishes confidently, answer guest questions accurately, and handle food-related situations the right way. Read through each section, then use the Scenarios tab as your on-the-floor reference.

Why does this matter?

A guest who asks "is this grilled or fried?" deserves a real answer, not a guess. A guest who says "this looks undercooked" needs you to know what to do immediately. A guest who asks "what is the sauce made of?" should feel like they are talking to someone who genuinely knows the food.

The two types of situation you will face

Guest questions — someone curious about how a dish is made, what an ingredient is, or why something looks a certain way. You answer confidently and helpfully.

Food complaints — something is wrong with the food. You apologise sincerely and escalate to the MOD and kitchen immediately. The only exceptions are missed items, delayed orders, and wrong orders — these you fix on the spot and then inform the MOD.

ESCALATION RULE: Missed items, delayed orders, wrong orders — fix immediately, inform MOD after. All food quality complaints, food safety concerns, and consistency issues — apologise and escalate to MOD and kitchen immediately. Never attempt to fix food yourself.

Heat and Cooking Methods

Every dish on the SOAK menu is cooked using one of these methods — or a combination. Knowing what each method does helps you describe a dish accurately and answer "how is this cooked?" with confidence.

🔥 Dry Heat Methods

No liquid involved — heat is applied directly to the food

Grilling

Food cooked over or under direct high heat. Creates char marks, a smoky flavour, and a crisp exterior. The inside stays juicy if cooked correctly.

At the table it looks like

Visible grill marks, slight char on the edges, sometimes a smoky aroma.

Used for

Grilled meats, fish, paneer, vegetables

Roasting

Food cooked uncovered in a hot oven. The dry heat creates a golden, caramelised exterior while the inside cooks through slowly.

At the table it looks like

Golden brown exterior, sometimes crispy edges. Often served with pan juices.

Used for

Whole chicken, lamb, root vegetables, potatoes

Frying

Food cooked in hot oil. Deep frying submerges the food completely — crispy exterior, cooked through. Shallow frying uses less oil and cooks one side at a time.

At the table it looks like

Golden, crispy, uniformly coloured on the outside. Served hot.

Used for

Fritters, cutlets, spring rolls, certain breads

Sautéing

Food cooked quickly in a small amount of hot oil or butter in a pan. Fast, high heat — preserves colour and texture.

At the table it looks like

Bright, slightly glossy, tender but not soft.

Used for

Vegetables, mushrooms, quick-cooked proteins

Baking

Food cooked in an enclosed oven. Used for bread, pastry, and dishes in a mould or dish.

At the table it looks like

Set, structured, often with a golden crust. Consistent texture throughout.

Used for

Bread, pasta bakes, gratins, pastry

💧 Wet Heat Methods

Liquid is involved — water, stock, or steam does the cooking

Steaming

Food cooked above boiling water — steam does the cooking without the food touching water. Preserves nutrients, colour, and delicate textures.

At the table it looks like

Moist, tender, bright in colour. No browning or char.

Used for

Dim sum, momos, fish, delicate vegetables

Poaching

Food gently cooked in liquid kept just below boiling point. Very gentle — produces a soft, delicate texture without any colour.

At the table it looks like

Pale, very soft, moist. No crust or char.

Used for

Eggs, fish, chicken, fruit

Braising

Food first seared in a pan then slow-cooked in a small amount of liquid in a covered pot. Makes tough cuts of meat incredibly tender.

At the table it looks like

Very tender, falling apart, rich deep colour. Usually served with the cooking liquid as a sauce.

Used for

Slow-cooked meats, lamb shanks, short ribs

On the floor: When a guest asks "how is this cooked?", use these methods. For example: "The fish is pan-sauteed — golden crust on the outside, moist inside." You do not need the exact recipe. Just the method is enough.

The Tandoor

The tandoor is one of the most distinctive elements of the SOAK kitchen. Guests who are unfamiliar with it will ask what it is. You should be able to explain it simply and confidently.

What is a tandoor?

A tandoor is a traditional clay oven, cylindrical in shape, heated from the inside using charcoal or wood fire. Temperatures inside a tandoor can reach 400 to 500 degrees Celsius — far hotter than a conventional oven. This extreme heat is what makes tandoor cooking unique.

How food is cooked in it

Meats and vegetables are threaded onto long metal skewers and lowered into the tandoor vertically. Breads like naan are slapped directly onto the inside walls of the oven, where they stick and cook in about 90 seconds.

What makes tandoor food distinctive

Slight char on the exterior Smoky, earthy aroma Juicy interior from fast, intense cooking Slightly crisp edges Distinctive flavour from the clay and charcoal

How to explain it to a guest

"The tandoor is a traditional clay oven that reaches extremely high temperatures — much hotter than a normal oven. The food cooks very quickly in the intense heat, which gives it that slightly charred exterior and smoky flavour while keeping the inside moist and juicy."

Common tandoor dishes

Tandoori chicken and meats

Marinated in spiced yoghurt then cooked on skewers in the tandoor. Juicy inside, slightly charred outside.

Seekh kebab

Minced meat mixed with spices, shaped around a flat skewer, and cooked in the tandoor. Soft, smoky, slightly crispy on the outside.

Paneer tikka

Cubes of fresh cottage cheese marinated in spiced yoghurt and cooked in the tandoor. Slightly charred outside, soft and warm inside.

Naan and tandoori roti

Dough slapped directly onto the inside wall of the tandoor. Cooks in about 90 seconds. Comes out with characteristic bubbles and slight char marks.

Guest asks: "Why is there char on my food — is it burnt?" Say: "That slight char is completely intentional — it is the signature of tandoor cooking. The extreme heat creates that smoky, slightly charred exterior while the inside stays perfectly cooked."

Stocks, Sauces and Bases

Stocks and sauces are the foundation of most dishes at SOAK. You do not need to know exact recipes — but you should know what these terms mean and how to describe them simply when a guest asks.

What is a stock?

A stock is a flavoured liquid made by simmering bones, vegetables, and aromatics in water for several hours. It is the base of most soups, sauces, and braises — it adds depth that plain water cannot.

Chicken stock

Light, mild, and versatile. Used in soups, risottos, and light sauces.

Beef stock

Richer and darker. Used in hearty meat dishes, gravies, and reductions.

Fish stock

Light and delicate. Used in seafood dishes and light cream sauces.

Vegetable stock

Made from vegetables only. Used in vegetarian dishes.

How to explain to a guest: "The base of that soup is a slow-cooked stock — bones and vegetables simmered for hours to build a deep, rich flavour."

What is a reduction?

A reduction is made by simmering a liquid — stock, wine, or sauce — uncovered over heat until some of the water evaporates. As it reduces, the liquid becomes thicker and the flavours become more concentrated and intense.

How to explain to a guest: "That sauce is a reduction — it starts as a liquid and is slowly cooked down until it becomes thick and concentrated. The flavour gets much more intense as it reduces."

What is a jus?

A jus (pronounced "zhoo") is the natural cooking juices from a roasted or braised meat, often reduced and refined into a light, intensely flavoured sauce. It is not thickened with flour or cream. Much lighter than a gravy but far more flavourful.

How to explain to a guest: "The jus is the natural juice from the meat as it cooked — collected and reduced into a light sauce. Much lighter than a gravy, but very intense in flavour."

Common sauce types

Cream sauce

Made from cream reduced with butter. Rich, smooth, and coating. Pale in colour. Used in pasta, fish, and chicken dishes.

Taste

Rich, slightly sweet, smooth.

Tomato-based sauce

Made from tomatoes cooked down with oil, garlic, and aromatics. Bright red. Used in pasta, pizzas, and braises.

Taste

Bright, slightly acidic, fresh.

Indian gravies

Built from a base of onion, tomato, ginger, and garlic cooked down with spices. Cream or yoghurt is sometimes added to finish.

Taste

Complex, spiced, and layered.

Pasta

SOAK's menu includes Italian-influenced pasta dishes. Guests will ask about shapes, what al dente means, and the difference between fresh and dried pasta.

Fresh pasta vs dried pasta

Fresh pasta

Made in-house from flour and eggs. Softer, silkier. Cooks in 2-3 minutes. Best with light, creamy sauces. Richer, eggy flavour.

How to explain

"This is freshly made pasta — softer and silkier, with a richer flavour from the eggs."

Dried pasta

Made from semolina and water. Firmer texture. Takes 8-12 minutes. Holds up better with bold, chunky sauces.

How to explain

"This is dried pasta — it has a firmer bite and works beautifully with a bold sauce like this."

What does al dente mean?

Al dente is Italian for "to the tooth." It describes pasta that is cooked through but still has a very slight firmness when you bite it — not hard, not soft, but with just a little resistance. This is the correct way to cook pasta in Italian cuisine.

How to explain to a guest: "Al dente means the pasta is cooked so it still has a very slight bite — not hard, but not soft either. It is the traditional Italian way and the texture holds up much better with the sauce."

If a guest says their pasta feels undercooked or raw — listen carefully. If they are genuinely unhappy, apologise and escalate to the MOD. Do not explain al dente as a justification for a complaint.

Pasta shapes

Tagliatelle, Fettuccine, and Pappardelle are all long, flat ribbon pastas — the only difference is width. Tagliatelle and Fettuccine are nearly identical at 6-8mm wide. Pappardelle is much wider at 16-20mm and suited to heavier, chunkier sauces.

Spaghetti

Long, thin, round strands. Classic with tomato, aglio e olio, or carbonara.

Tagliatelle

Long flat ribbons, 6-8mm wide. Usually fresh. Best with meat sauces or cream.

Fettuccine

Almost identical to tagliatelle — 6-7mm wide, slightly thicker and denser. The Roman version. Famous for fettuccine alfredo.

Pappardelle

Very wide flat ribbons, 16-20mm. Usually fresh. Pairs with rich, chunky meat sauces — bold enough to match the wide surface.

Penne

Short tubes with diagonal cut ends. Hollow centre holds sauce inside. Works with almost any sauce.

Rigatoni

Larger ridged tubes. Ridges and hollow centre catch chunky, hearty sauces well.

Fusilli

Short spirals. The twists trap sauce. Works well with pesto or tomato.

Farfalle

Bow-tie shape. Light and fun. Good with cream sauces and light vegetables.

Orecchiette

Small ear-shaped pasta. The cup holds chunky or vegetable-based sauces perfectly.

Ravioli

Filled pasta parcels — cheese, meat, or vegetables. Usually fresh. Best with butter or light sauces.

Linguine

Like spaghetti but flatter. Works well with seafood — classic with clams or prawns.

Gnocchi

Soft potato and flour dumplings. Very pillowy. Works with butter, tomato, or cream sauces.

Conchiglie

Shell-shaped pasta. The concave shape scoops up chunky sauces beautifully.

Bread

SOAK's menu spans Indian and international bread traditions. Guests will ask about the difference between naan and roti, or what sourdough is. These are easy questions to answer if you know the basics.

Indian breads

Naan

A leavened flatbread made from refined flour with yoghurt and sometimes egg. Cooked by being slapped onto the inner wall of a tandoor. Soft, slightly charred, and puffy with characteristic bubbles. Can be plain, buttered, or stuffed.

Taste and texture

Soft, slightly chewy, mild tangy flavour from the yoghurt. Slight char on the surface.

Tandoori Roti

Similar to naan but made from whole wheat flour without yeast — so it is not leavened. Also cooked in the tandoor. Healthier and slightly denser than naan.

Taste and texture

Slightly denser and earthier than naan. Nutty flavour from the whole wheat.

Roti / Chapati

A thin, unleavened whole wheat flatbread cooked on a flat griddle. Simple and light. The most everyday bread in Indian cooking.

Taste and texture

Very thin, soft, and mild. Slightly smoky if finished directly on the flame.

Paratha

A layered, flaky flatbread made from whole wheat flour, cooked on a griddle with butter or ghee. Can be plain or stuffed. Richer and more substantial than a roti.

Taste and texture

Flaky layers, buttery, and rich. More filling than a roti.

Kulcha

Similar to naan but softer and slightly thicker. Made from refined flour and often stuffed with onion, potato, or paneer. Cooked in the tandoor.

Taste and texture

Soft, pillowy, mild in flavour. The stuffing is the star.

Puri

A small, round, unleavened whole wheat bread that is deep fried. It puffs up into a hollow ball when it hits the hot oil. Must be eaten straight away.

Taste and texture

Crispy on the outside, soft inside. Light and airy.

South Indian Parotta

A layered, flaky flatbread made from maida (refined flour), cooked on a griddle with oil. Unlike the North Indian paratha which uses whole wheat flour, the South Indian parotta uses refined flour — giving it a distinctly lighter, more delicate layered texture. The layers are created by repeatedly folding and coiling the dough before cooking.

Taste and texture

Rich, slightly crispy on the outside, soft and flaky inside. More delicate than a paratha.

Guest asks: "What is the difference between naan and roti?" Say: "Naan is made from refined flour with yoghurt and cooked in the tandoor — softer and slightly richer. Roti is made from whole wheat flour on a flat griddle — lighter and simpler. Both are delicious but very different in texture and flavour."

Breads

Focaccia

A thick, flat Italian bread baked with olive oil and sea salt. Dimpled on top. Crispy on the outside, airy and soft inside. Can be topped with rosemary, olives, or tomatoes.

Taste and texture

Rich and savoury from the olive oil. Slightly crispy crust, soft open crumb inside.

Ciabatta

An Italian white bread with a very open, holey crumb and a thin, crispy crust. Long and flat. The holes make it excellent for dipping in oil or soaking up sauces.

Taste and texture

Mild, slightly chewy, crisp crust. Very versatile.

White bread

Made from refined white flour. Soft, light, and mild in flavour. A universally familiar bread with a fine, even crumb and a thin soft crust.

Taste and texture

Soft, mild, slightly sweet. Very approachable.

Multigrain bread

Made with a mix of grains and seeds — wheat, oats, flax, sunflower, and others. Denser and more nutritious than white bread. Visible seeds and grains throughout.

Taste and texture

Nutty, slightly earthy, hearty. More texture and flavour than white bread.

Sourdough

Made using a natural fermentation process — a "starter" of wild yeast and bacteria rather than commercial yeast. Takes much longer to make. Distinctive tangy flavour. Thick, crackling crust and an open, chewy crumb.

Taste and texture

Tangy, complex, slightly sour. Thick crust, chewy inside. Very satisfying.

Japanese milk bread

An extremely soft, pillowy white bread made using a technique called tangzhong — cooking a portion of the flour with water or milk into a paste before adding it to the dough. This creates an exceptionally soft, moist texture that stays fresh for longer.

Taste and texture

Very soft, slightly sweet, incredibly pillowy. Melts in the mouth. Unlike any other bread.

Flavour Principles

Understanding why food tastes the way it does makes you a much better communicator on the floor. There are four elements that create balanced, delicious food — salt, acid, fat, and heat.

The four elements of flavour

Salt

Salt does not just make food taste salty. It amplifies all other flavours and suppresses bitterness. A dish that tastes flat is often under-seasoned. Salt is used at every stage of cooking, not just at the end.

How to explain to a guest

"The seasoning is there to bring out all the other flavours — not to make it taste salty."

Acid

Acid — from lemon, lime, vinegar, or wine — adds brightness and contrast. It cuts through richness and makes a dish taste fresh. This is why lemon goes on fish, why vinegar goes in a dressing, why lime finishes a curry.

How to explain to a guest

"The lime in that dish balances the richness — it adds brightness that keeps the flavours fresh."

Fat

Fat — butter, oil, cream, ghee — carries flavour and adds richness. It coats the tongue and allows flavours to linger. Dishes without fat often taste thin and flat.

How to explain to a guest

"That richness comes from the butter — fat carries flavour and makes it feel smooth and full."

Heat

Heat transforms ingredients. It creates caramelisation (browning of sugars) and the Maillard reaction (browning of proteins — what gives grilled meat its flavour). The way heat is applied determines the entire character of a dish.

How to explain to a guest

"That golden crust is from caramelisation — when heat browns the surface, it creates a whole new layer of flavour."

Taste profile words — use these when describing food

Umami — deep, savoury, meaty richness Spicy — heat from chilli or pepper Bright — fresh, light, acidic Rich — heavy, full, coating Earthy — deep, grounding, slightly mineral Fresh — clean, light, not heavy Bitter — sharp, slightly astringent Sweet — naturally or added sugar Nutty — warm, toasted flavour Tangy — sour with brightness Smoky — charred, wood-fire flavour Creamy — smooth, rich, dairy-forward

Use two or three words when describing a dish. For example: "It is rich and slightly smoky with a bright citrus finish." That tells the guest exactly what to expect — in one sentence.

Allergen Awareness

Allergen handling is one of your most important responsibilities on the floor. Getting this wrong can cause serious harm to a guest. Treat every allergy with the utmost care, be completely honest, and always involve the MOD and kitchen when there is any doubt.

SOAK does not operate a fully certified allergen-free kitchen. Cross-contamination is possible. You must never make promises you cannot keep. When in doubt, protect the guest — not the sale.

The most common allergens to know

Gluten (wheat, barley, rye) Dairy (milk, cream, butter, cheese) Eggs Nuts (peanuts, tree nuts) Shellfish Fish Soy Sesame Sulphites (in wine and some sauces)

Where allergens commonly hide

Gluten

Soy sauce, marinades, gravies thickened with flour, certain spice mixes, fried foods (shared fryer oil)

Dairy

Marinades with yoghurt, sauces, butter finishing, naan, certain curries, desserts

Nuts

Chutneys, korma-style gravies, desserts, pesto, certain spice pastes

Eggs

Fresh pasta, mayonnaise, certain batters, some breads and pastries

The SOAK allergen protocol — step by step

1
When a guest mentions an allergy, acknowledge it immediately and take it seriously — every single time.
2
Be completely honest: "I want to make sure we handle this carefully for you — let me check with the kitchen."
3
Add a clear allergen note on every KOT for that guest. Write it prominently — for example: "GUEST ALLERGY: NUTS — NO NUTS IN ANY FORM."
4
Inform the MOD immediately via walkie talkie.
5
If there is a high chance of allergen traces in the dish, tell the guest frankly: "I want to be honest with you — this dish is prepared in a kitchen where [allergen] is used, and there is a real risk of traces. I would not recommend this dish for you." Do not soften this to make a sale.
6
Suggest a safer alternative if one exists.
7
If the guest has further questions or you are uncertain — call a senior or the MOD immediately. Do not guess.

Never tell a guest a dish is safe unless you are completely certain. If there is any doubt — say so. A guest with a serious allergy is trusting you with their health.

What cross-contamination means

Cross-contamination happens when an allergen from one food transfers to another — through shared equipment, shared oil, or shared surfaces. A guest with a severe nut allergy could react to even tiny traces from shared cooking equipment.

What to tell a guest if they ask

"Our kitchen handles a range of ingredients including [allergen]. While we take great care, I cannot guarantee there is zero risk of cross-contamination. If your allergy is severe, I would strongly advise caution and suggest something safer for you."

How to Describe a Dish You Haven't Tasted

You will not have tasted every dish on the menu. But you should be able to describe any dish confidently when a guest asks. Use four pieces of information from the Menu Training Reference.

The four-part description framework

1
What it is — the main ingredient and the broad category. "It is a grilled sea bass..." / "It is a slow-braised lamb shank..."
2
How it is cooked — the method. "...cooked in the tandoor..." / "...pan-seared and finished in the oven..."
3
What it tastes like — two or three taste profile words. "...smoky and slightly charred on the outside, juicy inside..."
4
What it comes with — the accompaniments. "...served with a mint chutney and sliced onion."

Example: "The paneer tikka is fresh cottage cheese marinated in a spiced yoghurt mix and cooked in the tandoor. It comes out slightly charred on the outside with a smoky flavour, soft and warm inside. It is served with a mint chutney." Two to three sentences. Confident, accurate, and helpful.

When you genuinely don't know something

Do not guess. Say: "That is a great question — let me check with the kitchen and come straight back to you." Come back with a real answer within two minutes.

Never say

"I think it has..." / "It might be..." / "I am pretty sure..." — these are guesses. A confident wrong answer damages trust far more than an honest "let me check."

Useful phrases for describing food

For texture

"Very tender" / "Falls apart" / "Crispy outside, soft inside" / "Smooth and creamy" / "Light and airy" / "Firm and satisfying"

For flavour

"Rich and earthy" / "Bright and citrusy" / "Smoky with a slight char" / "Mild and delicate" / "Bold and spiced" / "Sweet with a slight tang"

For cooking method

"Slow-cooked" / "Flash-grilled" / "Roasted until golden" / "Gently poached" / "Cooked in the tandoor" / "Pan-seared and finished in the oven"

For portion and format

"Great for sharing" / "A substantial main" / "Light enough as a starter" / "Comes in a warm bowl" / "Served on a wooden board"
